Sierra Leone Civil War (1991–2001)

The Sierra Leone Civil War lasted from 1991 to 2001 and was one of the most devastating conflicts in West African history. The war caused the deaths of at least 50,000 people and displaced nearly half of the country's population. The conflict was rooted in decades of corruption, poor governance, and dissatisfaction with the ruling elite following Sierra Leone's independence from Britain in 1961. Many people in the rural interior felt marginalized by the wealthy political elite concentrated in Freetown. Another major cause of the war was the struggle for control of Sierra Leone's valuable diamond resources, which became both a motivation for fighting and a source of funding for the conflict.

The war began when the Revolutionary United Front (RUF), led by former army sergeant Foday Sankoh, launched an attack against the government in 1991. Unlike many African conflicts driven by ethnic or ideological differences, the Sierra Leone conflict was largely centered on gaining control of diamond-mining regions. The RUF became notorious for brutal tactics, including the recruitment of child soldiers, widespread rape, sexual slavery, mutilation of civilians, and other atrocities. The conflict was closely linked to neighboring Liberia, whose president, Charles Taylor, supported the RUF by supplying weapons in exchange for diamonds. Taylor's involvement later led to war crimes charges against him.

The Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (AFRC), led by Major Johnny Paul Koroma, seized power in a military coup in 1997 and formed an alliance with the RUF. Together they controlled parts of the country until they were driven from power by the Economic Community of West African States Monitoring Group (ECOMOG), a regional peacekeeping force primarily composed of Nigerian troops. Another important group was the Kamajors, also known as the Civil Defense Forces (CDF), who fought alongside the government. Although they defended the government, some members were also accused of committing war crimes during the conflict.

The war had a devastating humanitarian impact. Nearly half of Sierra Leone's population was displaced, and hundreds of thousands sought refuge in neighboring countries. Approximately 100,000 people suffered amputations and other mutilations, particularly at the hands of rebel forces. Tens of thousands of children were forced to become soldiers, while many women and girls experienced sexual violence and enslavement. The country's economy, infrastructure, and social systems were severely damaged, leaving long-lasting effects on national development.

International efforts eventually helped bring an end to the conflict. The United Nations imposed restrictions on Sierra Leone's diamond exports to reduce funding for the rebels and deployed the United Nations Mission in Sierra Leone (UNAMSIL) to support peace efforts. Following the war, the Special Court for Sierra Leone was established to prosecute those responsible for serious war crimes. Several rebel leaders and militia commanders were convicted, while Charles Taylor was tried in The Hague for his role in supporting the conflict. The war officially ended in 2001, and democratic elections helped restore political stability under President Ahmad Tejan Kabbah. The conflict remains an important example of how corruption, resource wealth, and regional instability can combine to fuel a prolonged and destructive civil war.
